The best technical writing I've read has one thing in common: it treats the reader's time as more valuable than the writer's effort. Every sentence earns its place, or it doesn't appear.

That sounds obvious. In practice it's hard, because writing is also thinking. The first draft is where you discover what you believe. The second draft is where you decide what to keep.

## Clarity is a structural problem

Most unclear writing isn't unclear at the sentence level. It's unclear because the ideas aren't in the right order. The reader meets a concept before they have the context to understand why it matters.

The fix is almost always to restructure, not to reword. Move the explanation closer to the thing being explained. Introduce terms before you use them. Put the conclusion at the top, not the bottom — especially in technical documentation where readers are scanning.

## Specificity beats emphasis

When writers want to stress something important, they reach for emphasis: *really important*, **critical note**, warning blocks. These work once. Used repeatedly, they train the reader to ignore them.

A better approach: be specific. Instead of *this is very slow*, write *this adds 200ms to every request*. The reader understands the stakes without being told how to feel about them.

## Read it out loud

The simplest editing technique I know: read the draft aloud. Sentences that sound awkward usually are. Long, tangled constructions become obvious when you have to breathe through them. If you stumble, the reader will too.

Most writing benefits from being shorter. Cut the last paragraph first — it's often a restatement of what you already said.
